THALES OF MILETUS (c. 624–546 BCE) — LOGIKĒ I, the FIRST to walk logic into mathematics. Before him math was RECIPES (Egyptian/Babylonian 'to find the area, do this' — trusted because it works); Thales asked WHY MUST it be so, and answered by ARGUMENT — a chain of reasons true for EVERY case at once. ⚑ LIVE: Thales' Theorem — drag the point P around a semicircle, the inscribed angle ∠APB stays EXACTLY 90° for all P, with the deductive proof made visible (drop the radius OP → two isosceles triangles → base angles α+β=90° ⟹ ∠APB=90°, the 'must' not the 'does'). The five theorems traditionally his (diameter halves the circle; isosceles base angles equal; vertical angles equal; the semicircle right angle; ASA). THE MAN: predicted the solar eclipse of 28 May 585 BCE (it stopped a battle — an oldest precisely-datable event); measured a pyramid by its shadow (similar triangles); 'water is the arche' (first natural philosophy, explaining the world without gods). CONFIDENCE-TIERED (honest about an ancient figure): STRONG (the reputation — Aristotle/Herodotus/Proclus name him founder; the eclipse is astronomically fixed) / TRADITIONAL (the theorems — documented centuries later via Eudemus→Proclus) / DEBATED (whether he 'proved' formally, or deduction matured later and was projected back). ⚑ Gate kept on: Thales LEFT NO SURVIVING WRITING — the romance and the record don't perfectly align, and saying so is the point.
